# Enterprise Deal Pattern — Winning Complex, Multi-Stakeholder Sales

## Context

This pattern applies when:
- Deal ACV exceeds $100K annually
- Buying committee involves 5+ stakeholders across 3+ functions
- Sales cycle is expected to exceed 90 days
- Formal procurement process is anticipated
- Multiple competitive alternatives are being evaluated
- The decision requires executive-level approval

This is the highest-complexity selling motion. Success requires simultaneous
mastery of discovery, stakeholder management, competitive positioning, and
commercial negotiation across a timeline measured in months, not weeks.

---

## Challenge

Enterprise deals fail for specific, predictable reasons:

1. **Single-threading** — Entire deal depends on one contact who cannot drive consensus
2. **Premature solution presentation** — Demo before diagnosis creates feature wars
3. **Weak champion** — Internal advocate lacks power, influence, or motivation
4. **Economic buyer absence** — Decision maker is never engaged; deal dies in committee
5. **Unmanaged procurement** — Legal, security, and procurement surprises add months
6. **Competitive blind spot** — Competitor shapes criteria before you engage
7. **No compelling event** — No urgency means no decision timeline

---

## Strategy

### Core Strategic Principles

**Principle 1: Multi-Thread Early and Deep**
By Stage 2, establish relationships with minimum 5 stakeholders. By Stage 3, this
should be 7-10 across technical, business, and executive functions. Single-threaded
enterprise deals have a win rate below 15%.

**Principle 2: Diagnosis Before Prescription**
Invest 2-3x more time in discovery than in demo. Enterprise buyers value sellers
who understand their problems deeply. The Challenger Sale research shows that
commercial teaching during discovery differentiates more than product features.

**Principle 3: Champion as Force Multiplier**
Your champion is your primary asset. They sell when you are not in the room. Invest
heavily in champion identification, testing, coaching, and enablement. A validated
champion (passes access, influence, and motivation tests) is the single strongest
predictor of enterprise deal success.

**Principle 4: Mutual Action Plans as Commitment Devices**
A shared, date-bound plan creates mutual accountability. Buyer reluctance to commit
to a MAP is an early warning signal. Willingness to share a MAP is a buying signal.

**Principle 5: Compete on Problem Framing, Not Features**
The seller who defines the problem wins the deal. If the competitor defines the
problem, you are playing their game. Invest in commercial insights that reframe
the buyer's understanding of their challenge.

## Execution

### Phase 1: Account Research and Access (Weeks 1-3)

**Objective:** Gain access to the right stakeholders with a relevant hypothesis.

**Step 1: Account Research (2-4 hours)**
- Annual report, 10-K, earnings call transcripts (if public)
- Recent news: funding, acquisitions, leadership changes, strategic announcements
- Job postings: reveal strategic priorities and technology direction
- LinkedIn analysis: organizational structure, key decision makers
- Technology stack: BuiltWith, G2 Stack, ZoomInfo technographic data
- Current vendor relationships (who are we displacing?)

**Step 2: Develop Account Hypothesis**
Before outreach, develop a hypothesis about the buyer's challenges:
"Based on [research finding], I believe [company] is experiencing [challenge]
which costs them [estimated impact]. Our [capability] addresses this by [mechanism]."

**Step 3: Multi-Channel Access Campaign**
- Executive-level personalized email (hypothesis-driven, not product pitch)
- LinkedIn InMail or connection request with value-add content
- Warm introduction via mutual connection, investor, or partner
- Event-based engagement (conference, webinar, community)
- Champion referral from adjacent team or division

**Exit Criteria:** Discovery meeting scheduled with at least one stakeholder who
has authority to advance evaluation.

### Phase 2: Multi-Stakeholder Discovery (Weeks 3-8)

**Objective:** Build deep understanding of pain, stakeholders, and decision dynamics.

**Step 4: Initial Discovery Meeting**
- Use SPIN questioning framework (Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-Payoff)
- Focus on business impact, not technical requirements
- Identify additional stakeholders and request introductions
- Document MEDDPICC baseline scores

**Step 5: Stakeholder Mapping**
After each interaction, update:
- Power-Interest Grid position for each stakeholder
- Role classification (Economic Buyer, Champion, Technical Buyer, etc.)
- Individual priorities and concerns
- Relationship to other stakeholders (alliances, tensions)
- Engagement level (unaware, aware, engaged, sponsoring)

**Step 6: Expand Discovery to 3+ Stakeholders**
- Technical stakeholder: architecture, integration, security requirements
- Business stakeholder: outcomes, metrics, organizational impact
- Executive stakeholder: strategic alignment, budget, timeline

**Step 7: Champion Identification and Validation**
Test potential champion with three criteria:
- Access: Can they get you meetings with the economic buyer?
- Influence: Does their recommendation carry weight internally?
- Motivation: Do they have a personal win in this succeeding?

**Exit Criteria:** Pain quantified in business terms ($X impact), 5+ stakeholders
mapped, champion identified and validated, MEDDPICC score >12/24.

### Phase 3: Solution Validation (Weeks 8-14)

**Objective:** Prove solution fit and build the business case.

**Step 8: Tailored Demonstration**
- Demo ONLY capabilities that address discovered pain points
- Include technical and business stakeholders
- Use buyer's data, scenarios, and terminology where possible
- Capture feedback and objections in real-time

**Step 9: POC or Technical Evaluation (if required)**
- Define success criteria BEFORE the evaluation begins
- Scope to one use case (not every feature)
- Weekly check-ins with evaluation team
- Document wins as they occur

**Step 10: Business Case Co-Creation**
- Build ROI model with buyer-validated assumptions
- Quantify cost of current state (what they pay today)
- Quantify value of future state (what changes with your solution)
- Include risk mitigation value, not just revenue/cost impact
- Review with champion before presenting to economic buyer

**Exit Criteria:** Solution validated by technical and business stakeholders,
business case built with buyer-validated numbers, MEDDPICC score >16/24.

### Phase 4: Consensus and Commitment (Weeks 14-20)

**Objective:** Align all stakeholders and secure organizational commitment.

**Step 11: Executive Engagement**
- Schedule executive-to-executive meeting
- Present industry insight (Challenger teach moment)
- Connect solution to buyer's strategic priorities
- Request: "Based on our discussion, can we align on timeline?"

**Step 12: Stakeholder Alignment Workshop**
- Convene all relevant stakeholders (in-person if possible)
- Present solution, business case, and implementation plan
- Surface objections explicitly and address them
- Build consensus through shared understanding

**Step 13: Mutual Action Plan Creation**
- Create shared document with all remaining steps
- Assign owners (both buyer and seller) to each step
- Set dates for each milestone
- Include post-signature milestones (implementation kickoff)
- Review weekly

**Exit Criteria:** Economic buyer confirmed budget and timeline, mutual action plan
agreed and dated, all stakeholder objections addressed, MEDDPICC score >20/24.

### Phase 5: Commercial Execution (Weeks 20-26)

**Objective:** Navigate procurement and close the deal.

**Step 14: Proposal Delivery**
- Present proposal in person (not just email)
- Walk through each section, gauge reaction
- No pricing surprises (discussed framework during consensus phase)
- Include implementation plan and success metrics

**Step 15: Procurement Navigation**
- Engage procurement early with compliance documentation
- Provide pre-completed security questionnaire
- Offer joint redline session to accelerate legal review
- Maintain parallel channel to economic buyer for decision confirmation
- Give procurement a visible "win" (meaningful concession)

**Step 16: Negotiation**
- Follow concession strategy (pre-planned, diminishing, traded)
- Separate price negotiation from legal negotiation
- Use Getting to Yes framework (interests, not positions)
- Escalate deadlocks to executive sponsors (both sides)

**Step 17: Close**
- Confirm signature authority and process
- Provide all final documents in required formats
- Monitor signature completion daily
- Celebrate and communicate internally

**Exit Criteria:** Contract signed, PO generated, implementation kickoff scheduled.

### Phase 6: Transition (Post-Close)

**Step 18: Handoff to Customer Success**
- Joint meeting: AE, CSM, buyer PM, executive sponsor
- Transfer all account context (stakeholder map, pain points, success criteria)
- Define 30/60/90-day success milestones
- Schedule first QBR
- AE remains engaged for first 90 days (relationship continuity)

## Metrics

| Metric | Target | Measurement |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Enterprise Win Rate | 20-30% | Closed Won / (Closed Won + Closed Lost) |
| Average Sales Cycle | 90-150 days | Median days from S0 to Close |
| Multi-Threading Depth | 7+ contacts at close | CRM contact count on closed deals |
| MEDDPICC Score at Close | 20+/24 | Deal scoring in CRM |
| MAP Adoption | 80%+ of S3+ deals | % of deals with active MAP |
| Business Case Attachment | 70%+ of proposals | % of proposals with ROI model |
| Forecast Accuracy | 85%+ | Commit accuracy within +/-10% |

---

## Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Risk | Prevention |
|-------------|------|-----------|
| Single-threaded deal | Champion leaves, deal dies | Multi-thread by S2 |
| Demo before discovery | Feature comparison, not value selling | Mandate discovery first |
| No economic buyer access | Deal dies in committee | Champion must facilitate EB meeting |
| Proposal via email | No context, no objection handling | Present in person or video |
| Ignoring procurement | 30-60 day surprise at the end | Engage procurement at S3 |
| Over-discounting to close | Margin erosion, precedent setting | Pre-planned concession strategy |
| Skipping MAP | No mutual accountability, deal slips | MAP required for all S3+ deals |
