# Expansion Sale Pattern — Growing Revenue Within Existing Accounts

## Context

This pattern applies when:
- Customer is an existing, active user of your product
- Opportunity exists to increase revenue through upsell, cross-sell, or seat expansion
- Customer has demonstrated positive adoption and satisfaction signals
- Existing relationship and product usage provide a foundation for growth

Expansion selling is the highest-ROI sales motion in SaaS. Win rates for expansion
deals are typically 60-80% (vs. 20-30% for new business). Sales cycles are 30-50%
shorter. CAC is 3-5x lower. NRR above 100% — which drives the most efficient
growth in SaaS — is entirely driven by expansion revenue. Companies that excel at
expansion selling build compounding revenue machines.

---

## Challenge

### Why Expansion Sales Still Require Strategy

Despite structural advantages (existing relationship, known product, lower
switching cost), expansion deals fail for predictable reasons:

**1. Relationship Complacency**
"They already love us — they will buy whatever we recommend." This assumption
leads to weak discovery, lazy proposals, and surprised sellers when the customer
says no. Every expansion deal requires its own discovery, justification, and
stakeholder alignment.

**2. Single-Threaded Expansion**
The AE or CSM talks to one contact. That contact leaves, loses budget authority,
or simply cannot sponsor additional purchases. Multi-threading is as important in
expansion as in new business.

**3. Product Gaps That Block Expansion**
The customer hits a feature limitation in their current deployment. Their
dissatisfaction creates a ceiling — they will not invest more until the current
deployment is fully satisfying.

**4. Budget Ownership**
The person who approved the initial purchase may not control the budget for
expansion. Cross-sell into different departments means finding new budget owners.

**5. Value Realization Gap**
If the customer has not realized the value of their initial purchase, they will
not invest in more. Expansion depends on first making the initial deployment
successful.

---

## Strategy

### Expansion Selling Principles

**Principle 1: Value First, Then Expansion**
Never propose expansion until the current deployment is healthy. Adoption metrics,
satisfaction scores, and support ticket trends must be positive. Selling more to
an unhappy customer accelerates churn, not growth.

**Principle 2: Discover, Do Not Assume**
Even with an existing relationship, expansion requires discovery. The buyer's
situation has evolved since the initial purchase. New challenges, new stakeholders,
new priorities. Ask questions; do not extrapolate from old information.

**Principle 3: Land-and-Expand as Design Intent**
The initial sale should be designed to create expansion opportunities. This means:
- Pricing the initial deployment to demonstrate value at low risk
- Structuring the product to create natural expansion moments
- Building success metrics that reveal additional use cases
- Deploying with the team most likely to become internal champions

**Principle 4: Customer Success as Expansion Engine**
CSMs are the primary sensors for expansion signals. They see usage patterns,
hear feature requests, and detect organizational changes. The AE-CSM partnership
for expansion is the most important cross-functional collaboration in the revenue
organization.

## Expansion Types

### Type 1: Seat Expansion

**Trigger Signals:**
- Usage approaching license limit (>80% seat utilization)
- New team members asking for access
- Current users logging in significantly more than contract scope implies
- Manager requests bulk onboarding for additional team members

**Approach:**
- Frictionless: Enable self-serve seat addition with credit card
- Guided: CSM proactively reaches out when usage approaches threshold
- Negotiated: Larger seat expansion bundles with volume discount

**Pricing Strategy:**
- Same per-seat price as initial deployment (standard)
- Volume discount tiers (incentivize larger expansion)
- Enterprise agreement conversion (flat rate for organization-wide access)

### Type 2: Module/Feature Upsell

**Trigger Signals:**
- Customer support tickets requesting specific capabilities
- Product usage data showing exploration of premium features (trial clicks)
- Customer feedback during QBRs expressing unmet needs
- Industry or regulatory changes creating new requirements

**Approach:**
- Demonstrate the module/feature in context of their current workflow
- Build a mini business case specific to the additional capability
- Offer a time-limited trial or POC of the premium feature
- Show how existing customers with similar profiles use the module

**Pricing Strategy:**
- Additive pricing (current price + module price)
- Package upgrade (move to next tier that includes the module)
- Bundled discount (all modules at lower per-module price)

### Type 3: Cross-Sell to New Department

**Trigger Signals:**
- Internal referral from current user to colleague in another department
- Conference attendance from a new department within the account
- Organizational change that connects your champion to new teams
- Product use case that naturally spans departments

**Approach:**
- Ask current champion for introduction to adjacent department
- Position as "extending the success you have built" rather than a new purchase
- Tailor the value proposition to the new department's specific needs
- Offer the new department a proof of value (pilot, limited deployment)

**Pricing Strategy:**
- New department typically pays independently (separate cost center)
- Cross-sell discount to incentivize expansion (10-15% discount)
- Enterprise agreement that covers multiple departments (volume play)

### Type 4: Use Case Expansion

**Trigger Signals:**
- Customer discovers new application of existing product during usage
- Product release enables a capability the customer was not using for this purpose
- QBR conversation reveals adjacent problems your product can solve
- Success metrics from initial deployment exceed expectations

**Approach:**
- Share case studies of other customers using the product for this use case
- Offer guided exploration with SE or CSM support
- Build an implementation plan specific to the new use case
- Quantify additional value the new use case creates

### Type 5: Platform Consolidation

**Trigger Signals:**
- Customer expresses frustration with managing multiple vendors
- Cost-cutting initiative drives vendor consolidation
- New CTO or VP with platform preference
- Competitive displacement opportunity within the account

**Approach:**
- Position your product as the consolidation platform
- Build a TCO comparison: multiple point solutions vs. your platform
- Phase the consolidation over 12-18 months (manageable change)
- Offer migration support from displaced vendors

## Execution

### Step 1: Expansion Signal Detection

**Data-Driven Signals:**
| Signal | Source | Action |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Usage at >80% of license | Product analytics | CSM outreach for seat expansion |
| Premium feature trial clicks | Product analytics | CSM demonstration of premium |
| Support tickets for missing capability | Support system | AE/CSM discovery for upsell |
| NPS score > 50 | Survey system | Reference request + expansion conversation |
| QBR reveals new initiative | CSM notes | AE engagement for cross-sell |
| Executive change in account | LinkedIn alert | Executive alignment meeting |
| Renewal in 4+ months | CRM | Pre-renewal expansion packaging |

### Step 2: Expansion Discovery

Even with an existing relationship, conduct structured discovery:

**Expansion Discovery Questions:**
- "Since our last strategic conversation, what has changed in your priorities?"
- "Where are you seeing the most value from our product today?"
- "Where are you experiencing limitations or wishing you could do more?"
- "Are there other teams or departments that could benefit from what you have built?"
- "What new initiatives are coming in the next 6-12 months that might create
  additional needs?"
- "If you could wave a magic wand and change one thing about your current setup,
  what would it be?"

### Step 3: Expansion Proposal

**Proposal Structure:**
1. Value delivered (quantified) — what you have accomplished together
2. Current state assessment — where the customer is today
3. Expansion opportunity — what additional value is available
4. Business case — quantified ROI of the expansion
5. Implementation plan — how the expansion will be deployed
6. Pricing — transparent, tied to value, with options

### Step 4: Internal Champion Mobilization

Your current champion is your expansion amplifier. Equip them:
- Internal presentation summarizing value delivered and expansion opportunity
- Business case they can present to their leadership
- Reference stories from peer companies who expanded similarly
- Talking points for budget approval conversations

### Step 5: Close and Deploy

Expansion deals should close faster than new business:
- Existing MSA: no new legal review (typically)
- Existing procurement relationship: faster PO process
- Known implementation path: shorter deployment timeline
- Existing support relationship: faster issue resolution

## Metrics

| Metric | Target | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| NRR (Net Revenue Retention) | >110% | Primary expansion metric |
| Expansion Win Rate | 60-80% | Should be 2-3x new business win rate |
| Expansion Sales Cycle | 30-60 days | Should be 50% shorter than new business |
| Expansion ACV | 30-50% of initial ACV | Healthy expansion deal size |
| Customer Expansion Rate | 20-30% of base expand annually | % of customers who expand |
| CSM-Sourced Expansion Pipeline | >50% | CSM should source majority of expansion |
| Expansion CAC | <$1 for every $5 expanded | 5:1 or better expansion efficiency |
| Time to First Expansion | <12 months | First expansion from initial purchase |

---

## Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Risk | Prevention |
|-------------|------|-----------|
| Selling to unhappy customers | Accelerates churn, damages trust | Check health scores before proposing |
| Skipping discovery | Proposing wrong expansion type | Always discover current needs first |
| CSM-AE misalignment | Conflicting messages to customer | Joint planning, shared goals |
| Over-expanding too fast | Customer experiences adoption fatigue | Phase expansions, prove each before adding more |
| Ignoring competitive threats | Competitor displaces during expansion window | Monitor competitive signals in account |
| No executive relationship | Expansion blocked by budget authority gap | Build executive relationship before expansion pitch |
