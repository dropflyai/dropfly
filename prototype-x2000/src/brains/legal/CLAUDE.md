# PART II: CONTRACT LAW

## 2.1 Contract Formation

### Elements of a Valid Contract

| Element | Description | Key Issues |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| **Offer** | Manifestation of willingness to bargain | Must be definite; distinguished from invitation to deal |
| **Acceptance** | Manifestation of assent | Mirror image rule (common law) vs. UCC 2-207 |
| **Consideration** | Bargained-for exchange | Adequacy vs. sufficiency; past consideration invalid |
| **Capacity** | Legal ability to contract | Age, mental capacity, authority |
| **Legality** | Lawful purpose | Contracts for illegal purposes unenforceable |

### UCC vs. Common Law

| Aspect | Common Law | UCC Article 2 |
|--------|------------|---------------|
| **Applies to** | Services, real estate | Sale of goods |
| **Acceptance** | Mirror image rule | Accepts with different terms (2-207) |
| **Consideration for Mods** | Required | Not required (good faith) |
| **Statute of Frauds** | $500 | $500 for goods |
| **Gap Fillers** | Minimal | Extensive (price, delivery, payment) |

### Key Contract Clauses

| Clause | Purpose | Drafting Considerations |
|--------|---------|------------------------|
| **Limitation of Liability** | Cap damages | Typically 12 months fees; carve-outs for IP, gross negligence |
| **Indemnification** | Risk allocation | Scope, caps, procedures, insurance requirements |
| **Termination** | Exit rights | For convenience vs. for cause; wind-down obligations |
| **Assignment** | Transfer restrictions | Consent requirements; change of control |
| **Force Majeure** | Excuse for extraordinary events | Definition, notice requirements, duration limits |

# PART III: INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY

## 3.1 IP Framework Overview

| IP Type | What It Protects | Duration | Registration |
|---------|------------------|----------|--------------|
| **Patents** | Novel, useful, non-obvious inventions | 20 years from filing | Required (USPTO) |
| **Copyrights** | Original works of authorship | Life + 70 years | Automatic (registration for enforcement) |
| **Trademarks** | Source identifiers | Indefinite (with use) | Not required but advisable |
| **Trade Secrets** | Valuable confidential information | Indefinite (while secret) | No registration |

## 3.2 Patent Law

### Patentability Requirements

| Requirement | Meaning | Common Issues |
|-------------|---------|---------------|
| **Novel (35 USC 102)** | Not disclosed in prior art | Public use, publication, prior patents |
| **Non-obvious (35 USC 103)** | Not obvious to PHOSITA | Hindsight bias; secondary considerations |
| **Useful (35 USC 101)** | Practical utility | Abstract ideas, laws of nature (Alice/Mayo) |
| **Enabled** | Specification teaches how to make/use | Undue experimentation |
| **Written Description** | Inventor possessed invention | New matter; adequate support |

### Post-Alice Software Patent Analysis

**Alice v. CLS Bank (2014)** two-step test:

1. **Step 1:** Is the claim directed to an abstract idea?
2. **Step 2A:** Does it add "significantly more" (inventive concept)?

**Safe Harbors:**
- Specific technical improvements
- Novel hardware implementations
- Particular algorithms with concrete applications

## 4.1 Privacy Frameworks Comparison

### GDPR vs. CCPA/CPRA

| Aspect | GDPR | CCPA/CPRA |
|--------|------|-----------|
| **Scope** | All EU residents | CA residents; $25M+ revenue or 50K+ records |
| **Consent Model** | Opt-in required | Opt-out (Do Not Sell) |
| **Legal Bases** | 6 required bases | Not required |
| **Data Subject Rights** | Access, deletion, portability, rectification, restriction, object | Know, delete, opt-out, non-discrimination |
| **DPO Required** | Yes (certain processors) | No |
| **Breach Notification** | 72 hours to authority | "Most expedient time possible" |
| **Penalties** | Up to 4% global revenue or €20M | $7,500/intentional violation |
| **Private Right of Action** | Limited | Yes (data breaches) |

### Privacy by Design Principles (Cavoukian)

1. Proactive not reactive
2. Privacy as default
3. Privacy embedded into design
4. Full functionality (positive-sum)
5. End-to-end security
6. Visibility and transparency
7. Respect for user privacy

## 4.2 GDPR Deep Dive

### Lawful Bases for Processing (Art. 6)

| Basis | When Appropriate |
|-------|------------------|
| **Consent** | Freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous |
| **Contract** | Necessary for contract performance |
| **Legal Obligation** | Required by law |
| **Vital Interests** | Life-threatening situations |
| **Public Task** | Public interest or official authority |
| **Legitimate Interest** | Balanced against data subject rights |

### Data Subject Rights

| Right | Requirement |
|-------|-------------|

| **Portability (Art. 20)** | Machine-readable format; direct transfer |
| **Object (Art. 21)** | Object to processing; must stop unless compelling grounds |

### International Data Transfers

| Mechanism | Status |

### Why Delaware?

1. **Developed Case Law** — Over 100 years of corporate precedent
2. **Court of Chancery** — Expert judges, no juries, fast decisions
3. **Business-Friendly** — Flexible corporate statute
4. **Predictability** — Known outcomes
5. **VC Expectation** — Standard for institutional investment

## 5.2 Fiduciary Duties

### Director Fiduciary Duties

| Duty | Standard | Key Cases |
|------|----------|-----------|
| **Care** | Informed decision-making; gross negligence standard | Smith v. Van Gorkom (1985) |
| **Loyalty** | No self-dealing; corporate opportunity doctrine | Guth v. Loft (1939) |
| **Good Faith** | Subjective honesty; no conscious disregard | In re Caremark (1996) |

### Business Judgment Rule

**Presumption:** Directors acted on an informed basis, in good faith, and in the honest belief that action was in the corporation's best interest.

**Effect:** Court will not substitute its judgment if there is any rational business purpose.

**Rebuttal:** Self-dealing, lack of independence, gross negligence, bad faith.

### Entire Fairness Review

**Triggered by:** Self-dealing or conflicted transactions

**Two Prongs:**
1. **Fair Dealing** — Process (timing, initiation, negotiation, approval)
2. **Fair Price** — Economically fair to minority

# PART VIII: 20 YEARS EXPERIENCE — CASE STUDIES

## Case Study 1: The SCO v. IBM Linux Lawsuit

**Context:** SCO claimed IBM contributed Unix code to Linux (2003-2016).

**What Happened:**
- SCO claimed $5B in damages
- IBM countersued for patent infringement
- SCO bankrupted during litigation
- Most claims dismissed; IBM largely prevailed

**Legal Lessons:**
- Copyright ownership must be clear (SCO's chain of title was questionable)
- Open source contribution policies matter
- Litigation can destroy a company regardless of merit

**Lesson:** Clean IP provenance is essential. Document copyright ownership and contribution rights clearly.

---

## Case Study 2: Oracle v. Google (API Copyright)

**Context:** Oracle sued Google for copying Java API structure in Android (2010-2021).

**What Happened:**
- District court: APIs not copyrightable
- Federal Circuit: Reversed (APIs copyrightable)
- Supreme Court (2021): Fair use (transformative)

**Legal Lessons:**
- API copyrightability remains contested
- Fair use is powerful but unpredictable
- Reimplementation of APIs is risky

**Lesson:** API compatibility has legal risk. Document your clean-room implementation process.

---

## Case Study 3: Uber's Trade Secret Disaster (Waymo v. Uber)

**Context:** Waymo sued Uber for trade secret theft via employee Anthony Levandowski (2017).

**What Happened:**
- Levandowski downloaded 14,000 files before leaving Google
- Uber acquired Levandowski's startup
- Settlement: Uber paid $245M in equity; redesigned LIDAR
- Levandowski criminally convicted (later pardoned)

**Legal Lessons:**
- Employee transition procedures matter
- Due diligence must include IP hygiene
- Criminal liability is real for trade secret theft

**Lesson:** Exit interviews and technology audits are critical. What employees take can create massive liability.

---

## Case Study 4: Facebook/Cambridge Analytica (Privacy)

**Context:** Cambridge Analytica harvested 87M Facebook profiles without consent (2018).

**What Happened:**
- Developer API allowed data collection of users and their friends
- Data used for political targeting
- FTC $5B fine (largest privacy fine ever)
- Multiple investigations globally

**Legal Lessons:**
- Third-party data access creates massive risk
- "Consent" to terms is not blank-check authorization
- Privacy failures have regulatory, reputational, and legal consequences

**Lesson:** API access to user data must be strictly controlled. Friend-of-friend data sharing is dangerous.

---

## Case Study 5: Theranos Fraud

**Context:** Blood-testing startup defrauded investors and patients (2003-2018).

**What Happened:**
- Claimed revolutionary blood testing technology
- Technology never worked as claimed
- $700M+ raised from investors
- Holmes convicted on 4 fraud counts; 11-year sentence

**Legal Lessons:**
- Securities fraud includes omissions, not just lies
- Statements to investors create liability
- "Fake it till you make it" has limits

**Lesson:** Statements to investors must be truthful. Aspirational language must be clearly distinguished from current capability.

---

## Case Study 6: Airbnb Regulatory Navigation

**Context:** Short-term rental platform faced regulatory opposition in major cities.

**What Happened:**
- Cities banned or restricted short-term rentals
- Airbnb challenged laws, lobbied, and negotiated
- Now works with 1,000+ governments worldwide
- Occupancy taxes, registration, and limits accepted

**Legal Lessons:**
- Regulatory compliance can become competitive advantage
- Early dialogue better than confrontation
- Platform liability continues to evolve

**Lesson:** Proactive regulatory engagement is better than litigation. Working with regulators builds sustainable business.

---

## Case Study 7: Apple v. Epic Games (App Store)

**Context:** Epic challenged App Store 30% commission as antitrust violation (2020-2021).

**What Happened:**
- Epic intentionally violated App Store rules
- Apple removed Fortnite; Epic sued
- Mixed verdict: Apple won on antitrust; lost on anti-steering
- Injunction allows external payment links

**Legal Lessons:**
- Platform terms can be anticompetitive even if not illegal monopoly
- Contract violations for litigation strategy is risky
- Developer relations matter

**Lesson:** Platform power has limits. Anti-steering provisions are vulnerable.

---

## Case Study 8: Coinbase and Securities Law

**Context:** SEC alleged Coinbase operated unregistered securities exchange (2023).

**What Happened:**
- SEC sued after denying Coinbase petition for rulemaking
- Claimed multiple tokens are securities
- Coinbase argues reasonable reliance on prior guidance
- Litigation ongoing

**Legal Lessons:**
- Novel assets may be securities under Howey
- SEC enforcement is the de facto regulatory regime
- "Investment contract" analysis applies to crypto

**Lesson:** If return depends on others' efforts, it may be a security. Crypto exchanges face existential regulatory risk.

---

## Case Study 9: Microsoft/LinkedIn Data Scraping

**Context:** HiQ scraped LinkedIn data; LinkedIn tried to block (2017-2022).

**What Happened:**
- LinkedIn sent cease-and-desist
- HiQ sued for tortious interference
- 9th Circuit: Publicly available data can be scraped (CFAA)
- Supreme Court vacated; 9th Circuit reaffirmed

**Legal Lessons:**
- Public data scraping may not violate CFAA
- Terms of service enforcement limited
- Bots/crawlers in legal gray zone

**Lesson:** Public-facing data has limited legal protection. Design systems assuming data will be scraped.

---

## Case Study 10: Bankruptcy and IP Licenses (Toys "R" Us)

**Context:** How IP licenses behave in bankruptcy.

**What Happened:**
- Licensors can reject executory contracts in bankruptcy
- 365(n) allows licensees to retain rights (with conditions)
- Trademark licenses historically had no protection
- Recent amendments provide some protection

**Legal Lessons:**
- IP licenses are vulnerable in licensor bankruptcy
- Source code escrow doesn't guarantee access
- Due diligence must include licensor financial health

**Lesson:** Critical IP licenses need protective provisions. Consider escrow, step-in rights, and bankruptcy-specific terms.

# PART IX: 20 YEARS EXPERIENCE — FAILURE PATTERNS

## Failure Pattern 1: "Handshake Deals"

**Pattern:**
- Founders agree on equity split verbally
- No written agreements
- Disputes arise when company has value

**Warning Signs:**
- "We'll figure out the paperwork later"
- Co-founders contributing unequally
- No vesting on founder shares

**Consequences:**
- Litigation over ownership
- Deal-killing for investors
- Company dissolution

**Prevention:**
- Written agreements before any work
- Vesting for all founders
- Clear IP assignment

---

## Failure Pattern 2: "The Contractor Who Owns Your IP"

**Pattern:**
- Contractor develops core technology
- No work-for-hire agreement
- Copyright vests in contractor

**Warning Signs:**
- Using contractors for core development
- Master services agreement but no IP assignment
- Contractor using their own templates

**Consequences:**
- Contractor owns copyright
- License needed to use own product
- Due diligence failure blocks funding

**Prevention:**
- IP assignment in every contractor agreement
- Work-for-hire where applicable
- Employee for core technology

---

## Failure Pattern 3: "Open Source Contamination"

**Pattern:**
- Developer uses GPL code
- Code merged into proprietary product
- Copyleft requires disclosure

**Warning Signs:**
- No dependency auditing
- "Just grab it from GitHub"
- No open source policy

**Consequences:**
- Proprietary code must be disclosed
- Product redesign required
- Due diligence failure

**Prevention:**
- License scanning tools (FOSSA, Snyk)
- Open source policy
- Approval process for new dependencies

---

## Failure Pattern 4: "Terms of Service Liability Sink"

**Pattern:**
- Copy competitor's ToS
- Include provisions that don't apply
- Miss required disclosures

**Warning Signs:**
- ToS from template without review
- Not updated for product changes
- Missing regulatory requirements

**Consequences:**
- Regulatory violations (COPPA, HIPAA)
- Unenforceable arbitration clauses
- Class action exposure

**Prevention:**
- Custom ToS for business model
- Regular updates as product evolves
- Compliance review for each jurisdiction

---

## Failure Pattern 5: "Cap Table Chaos"

**Pattern:**
- Informal equity promises
- Missing option paperwork
- 409A violations

**Warning Signs:**
- "I told her she'd get 2%"
- Options granted without board approval
- No 409A valuation

**Consequences:**
- Tax liability for employees
- Invalid option grants
- Due diligence nightmare

**Prevention:**
- Formal board approval for all grants
- Annual 409A valuations
- Cap table software (Carta, Pulley)

# PART XI: 20 YEARS EXPERIENCE — WAR STORIES

## War Story 1: "The Missing Founder IP Assignment"

**Trigger:** Series A due diligence

**Story:**
Second co-founder had left a year earlier. Amicable departure, but no IP assignment was signed. He'd written 40% of the code. Now he wanted $1M for assignment. Investors walked. Company eventually paid $500K to close the gap.

**What We Learned:**
IP assignment at termination is non-negotiable. We now have a termination checklist that includes IP assignment execution before final payment.

---

## War Story 2: "The GPL Surprise"

**Trigger:** Acquisition due diligence

**Story:**
Target had clean proprietary product—or so they thought. License scan found GPL v3 code in core authentication module. Entire module would need to be disclosed or rewritten. Deal repriced by $5M. Took 6 months to rewrite.

**What We Learned:**
License scanning happens before every material transaction. We now run scans quarterly and before any funding round.

---

## War Story 3: "The 'Permanent' License"

**Trigger:** Vendor bankruptcy

**Story:**
Critical infrastructure software vendor went bankrupt. We had "perpetual license." Buyer rejected our license; demanded new agreement at 10x the price. No source code escrow. We had 90 days to find alternative or pay.

**What We Learned:**
Perpetual licenses aren't perpetual in bankruptcy. Critical vendor licenses now include escrow, step-in rights, and bankruptcy-specific terms.

---

## War Story 4: "The Privacy Promise"

**Trigger:** Feature launch

**Story:**
Marketing wanted to email users about new feature. Privacy policy said "we won't contact you about marketing." Legal said no. Marketing sent anyway. 50,000 GDPR complaints. €2M fine. CMO fired.

**What We Learned:**
Privacy policy is binding. Marketing must clear campaigns through legal. We now have consent management platform and privacy review for all communications.

---

## War Story 5: "The Oral Equity Promise"

**Trigger:** Key employee departure

**Story:**
CEO had promised early employee "you'll get equity." No documentation. No board approval. No 409A. Employee left, sued for 5% of company. Settled for $2M in cash because proving the negative was impossible.

**What We Learned:**
Equity promises must be in writing with board approval. All equity discussions now go through formal process. No exceptions.
