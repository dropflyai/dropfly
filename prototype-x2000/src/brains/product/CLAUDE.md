### Clayton Christensen — Jobs to Be Done

**Background:** Harvard Business School Professor (1952-2020); author of *The Innovator's Dilemma* (1997)

**Key Work:** *Competing Against Luck* (2016) — with Karen Dillon, David Duncan, Taddy Hall

**Core Framework — Jobs to Be Done:**
```
"Customers don't buy products. They hire them to get a job done."
```

**Job Dimensions:**
1. **Functional** — The practical task to accomplish
2. **Social** — How others perceive them
3. **Emotional** — How they want to feel

**Classic Example — The Milkshake Study:**
- "Why are half of all milkshakes sold before 8 a.m.?"
- Answer: Customers "hiring" milkshakes to make boring commute interesting, keep them full until lunch, be easy to consume while driving
- Competitors: Bananas, donuts, bagels (not other milkshakes)

**Key Insight:** "Innovation becomes more predictable when you focus on the job the customer is trying to accomplish."

### Tony Ulwick — Outcome-Driven Innovation

**Background:** Pioneer of JTBD (invented 1990); founder/CEO of Strategyn

**Core Framework — Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI):**

**The Opportunity Algorithm:**
```
Opportunity = Importance + (Importance - Satisfaction)

Where:
  Opportunity Score = How valuable is this to address?
  Importance = How important is this outcome to the customer?
  Satisfaction = How satisfied are they with current solutions?
```

**Customer Needs Framework:**
1. Core functional job
2. Desired outcomes (100+ per job)
3. Related jobs
4. Emotional and social jobs
5. Consumption chain jobs
6. Financial outcomes

**Track Record:** 86% success rate across hundreds of Fortune 1000 initiatives

**Key Insight:** "The key to innovation is not listening to what customers say, but understanding what outcomes they are trying to achieve."

### Gibson Biddle — DHM Model

## 1.3 Prioritization Frameworks

### RICE Scoring (Intercom)

**Formula:**
```
         Reach × Impact × Confidence
RICE = ────────────────────────────────
                  Effort
```

| Component | Definition | Scale |
|-----------|------------|-------|
| **Reach** | How many people affected per quarter? | Number |
| **Impact** | How much will it move metrics? | 3=massive, 2=high, 1=medium, 0.5=low, 0.25=minimal |
| **Confidence** | How certain are estimates? | 100%, 80%, 50%... |
| **Effort** | Person-months required | Number |

---

### ICE Scoring (Sean Ellis)

**Formula:**
```
ICE = Impact × Confidence × Ease
```
- Each component scored 1-10
- Simpler than RICE, good for rapid prioritization

---

### Kano Model (Noriaki Kano, 1984)

**Five Categories:**

| Category | Description | Over Time |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Must-Be (Basic)** | Expected; absence causes dissatisfaction | Stay basic |
| **One-Dimensional** | Linear relationship with investment | Stay linear |
| **Attractive (Delighters)** | Unexpected "wow" features | Become expected |
| **Indifferent** | Features users don't care about | Remove |
| **Reverse** | Features that reduce satisfaction | Remove |

**Key Insight:** Delighters become expected over time. Must constantly innovate.

---

### Stage-Gate Process (Robert Cooper, 1980s)

**Structure:**
```
Stage 1    Gate 1    Stage 2    Gate 2    Stage 3    Gate 3    Stage 4    Gate 4    Stage 5
Discovery ──▶──── Scoping ──▶──── Build ──▶──── Development ──▶──── Testing ──▶──── Launch
                                 Case                              Validation
```

**At Each Gate:**
- Go/Kill decision
- Cross-functional review
- Resource allocation

**Adoption:** ~80% of North American companies

## 1.4 Key Metrics Frameworks

### Sean Ellis PMF Test (40% Test)

**Survey Question:** "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?"

**Options:**
- Very disappointed
- Somewhat disappointed
- Not disappointed

**Benchmark:** 40%+ responding "Very disappointed" = Product-Market Fit achieved

**Research:** Companies below 40% struggled to gain traction; those above 40% experienced rapid growth

---

### AARRR Pirate Metrics (Dave McClure, 2007)

```
A ────────▶ A ────────▶ R ────────▶ R ────────▶ R
Acquisition  Activation  Retention   Referral    Revenue
   │            │           │           │           │
   ▼            ▼           ▼           ▼           ▼
How do     Do they     Do they      Do they    Do they
users      get value   come back?   tell       pay?
find us?   quickly?                 others?
```

**Key Insight:** Most early-stage startups should focus on retention and activation first, not acquisition.

---

### LTV:CAC Ratio

**Definitions:**
- **LTV (Lifetime Value):** Total profit from customer over relationship
- **CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost):** Total cost to acquire customer

**Benchmarks:**

| Ratio | Interpretation |
|-------|----------------|
| 3:1+ | Sustainable, efficient growth |
| 4:1+ | Great business model (B2B SaaS benchmark) |
| 5:1+ | Potentially under-investing in growth |
| <2:1 | Unsustainable long-term |
| 1:1 | Break-even (very bad) |

# PART II: DISCOVERY METHODS

## 2.1 Customer Discovery Interviews

### Steve Blank's Method

**Core Principle:** Ask about specifics in the past, not generics about the future

**Good Questions:**
- "Tell me about the last time you tried to..."
- "What happened next?"
- "What have you tried to solve this?"
- "What does that cost you (time, money, pain)?"

**Bad Questions:**
- "Would you use a product that..."
- "How much would you pay for..."
- "Do you think this is a good idea?"

**Critical Stat:** 42% of B2B products fail due to lack of market fit; 6/10 product launches fail to meet expectations

---

## 2.2 Jobs to Be Done Interviews

### Switch Interview Framework

**Focus on:** Moments when customers switch from old solution to new

**Four Forces:**
1. **Push of current situation** — What's not working now?
2. **Pull of new solution** — What's attractive about the alternative?
3. **Anxiety of change** — What worries them about switching?
4. **Habit of current** — What keeps them using what they have?

**Timeline:** Walk through the entire journey from first thought to purchase

---

## 2.3 Prototype Testing

### Fidelity Levels

| Level | Purpose | When to Use |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Paper sketches | Concept validation | Very early |
| Wireframes | Flow validation | Early discovery |
| Clickable prototype | Usability testing | Mid discovery |
| Functional MVP | Value validation | Late discovery |

**Key Principle:** Match fidelity to uncertainty. Don't build high-fidelity when low-fidelity would answer the question.

## 2.4 Cohort Analysis

**Types:**
- **Acquisition cohorts** — Grouped by sign-up date
- **Behavioral cohorts** — Grouped by specific actions
- **Revenue cohorts** — Grouped by spending patterns

**Key Metrics:**
- N-Day retention (Day 1, Day 7, Day 30)
- Rolling retention
- DAU/MAU ratio

**Benchmark:** Average app loses 80%+ of users within first month

## 3.3 Four Fits Framework (Brian Balfour)

**Four Fits Must Align:**

```
Market-Product Fit ◄──────────► Product-Channel Fit
        │                               │
        │                               │
        ▼                               ▼
Channel-Model Fit ◄──────────► Model-Market Fit
```

1. **Market-Product Fit:** Product solves real problem for specific market
2. **Product-Channel Fit:** Product suits available acquisition channels
3. **Channel-Model Fit:** CAC from channels supports business model
4. **Model-Market Fit:** Business model works for market characteristics

**Key Insight:** PMF is necessary but not sufficient. All four fits must align.

# PART IV: OPERATIONAL PROTOCOLS

## 4.1 Product Modes (MANDATORY)

One mode MUST be declared at the start of every product task.

### MODE_0_TO_1
- Finding product-market fit
- High uncertainty, rapid learning
- Metrics: Sean Ellis 40% test, retention curves
- Focus: Discovery over delivery

### MODE_1_TO_N
- Scaling proven product
- Lower uncertainty, growth focus
- Metrics: LTV:CAC, revenue, market share
- Focus: Optimization and expansion

### MODE_PLATFORM
- Building for ecosystem/developers
- API products, integrations
- Metrics: Developer adoption, ecosystem health
- Focus: Developer experience

### MODE_ENTERPRISE
- B2B with long sales cycles
- Stakeholder complexity
- Metrics: ACV, NRR, logo retention
- Focus: Customer success, account expansion

---

## 4.2 Decision Quality Framework

Before any product recommendation, apply:

| Lens | Question |
|------|----------|
| **Customer Impact** | How many customers affected? How severely? |
| **Business Value** | Revenue, retention, or strategic impact? |
| **Feasibility** | Engineering effort? Data/infra/skills available? |
| **Reversibility** | One-way door or two-way door? |
| **Strategic Alignment** | Advances North Star? Fits vision? |

---

## 4.3 PRD Quality Checklist

Every PRD must include:

1. **Problem Statement** — What problem are we solving? For whom?
2. **Success Metrics** — How will we measure success?
3. **User Stories** — What will users be able to do?
4. **Scope Definition** — What is IN scope? What is OUT?
5. **Technical Considerations** — Dependencies, constraints
6. **Risks and Mitigations** — What could go wrong?
7. **Timeline and Milestones** — When will we deliver?
8. **Kill Criteria** — When do we stop or pivot?

---

## 4.4 Experiment Protocol

Before running experiments:

1. **Hypothesis** — "We believe [change] will [outcome] because [reason]"
2. **Primary Metric** — What exactly are we measuring?
3. **Sample Size** — How many users needed for significance?
4. **Duration** — Minimum 2 weeks (capture weekly cycles)
5. **Success Criteria** — What constitutes a win?
6. **Guardrail Metrics** — What must NOT get worse?

# PART V: 20 YEARS EXPERIENCE — CASE STUDIES

## Case Study 1: The Pivot That Saved Slack

**Situation:** Tiny Speck building multiplayer game Glitch (2009-2012). Game failing to gain traction.

**Insight:** Internal communication tool team built for themselves was more valuable than the game.

**Pivot:** Killed Glitch, focused entirely on communication tool. Launched Slack (2013).

**Result:** 8,000 users day one → 15,000 by week two → $27.7B acquisition by Salesforce (2021).

**Lesson:** Pay attention to tools you build internally. Sometimes the side project IS the product.

---

## Case Study 2: The Feature That Killed Engagement

**Situation:** Social product added "read receipts" feature. Users requested it heavily in surveys.

**Result:** Message volume dropped 35%. Users stopped sending casual messages because receipts created obligation pressure.

**Root Cause:** Users said they wanted visibility; reality was it created anxiety.

**Resolution:** Made read receipts optional (off by default). Engagement recovered.

**Lesson:** What users say they want may not match what they actually need. Observe behavior, not just feedback.

---

## Case Study 3: The A/B Test That Saved $10M

**Situation:** E-commerce team about to build recommendation engine. 6-month project, $2M investment.

**Alternative:** PM proposed simple "customers also bought" widget. 2-week build.

**Test:** Simple widget vs. control. Conversion lift 18%.

**Outcome:** Cancelled complex recommendation engine. Simple solution shipped in 2 weeks for $50K.

**Lesson:** Always test simple solutions first. Complex is not always better.

---

## Case Study 4: The Enterprise Deal That Changed Everything

**Situation:** B2B SaaS focused on SMB customers. $29/month pricing.

**Opportunity:** Fortune 500 company offered $500K/year contract but needed specific features.

**Decision:** Built enterprise features. Signed deal.

**Consequence:** SMB product neglected. Enterprise features added complexity. NPS dropped. SMB churn increased.

**Resolution:** Spun out enterprise product as separate offering with dedicated team.

**Lesson:** Enterprise and SMB products are different businesses. Don't let one customer distort product direction.

---

## Case Study 5: The Launch That Broke Everything

**Situation:** Team shipped major redesign. 6 months of work. Full rollout day one.

**Result:** Support tickets 400% above normal. Power users threatening to cancel. Social media backlash.

**Root Cause:** No beta program. No gradual rollout. No user research on redesign.

**Resolution:** Offered "classic" mode for 6 months. Gradually migrated users with training.

**Lesson:** Never ship major changes to 100% of users on day one. Always beta test. Always have rollback plan.

---

## Case Study 6: The Metric That Misled

**Situation:** Product team optimizing for "daily active users" (DAU). Shipped notification spam to increase DAU.

**Result:** DAU increased 20%. But monthly active users (MAU) dropped 15%. Users uninstalling.

**Root Cause:** Optimizing proxy metric instead of actual value. DAU manipulable; user satisfaction not.

**Resolution:** Changed primary metric to "weekly users who complete core action."

**Lesson:** Choose metrics carefully. Bad metrics drive bad behavior.

---

## Case Study 7: The Platform Bet That Paid Off

**Situation:** Startup building API product. Investors pushing for direct-to-customer product.

**Decision:** Stayed with API strategy. Built developer ecosystem instead.

**Challenge:** Slower growth initially. Investors impatient.

**Result:** Developer network effects kicked in. Partners building on platform became distribution channel. Ultimately more defensible than direct product.

**Lesson:** Platform strategies take longer but create stronger moats. Requires investor alignment and patience.

---

## Case Study 8: The Pricing Experiment

**Situation:** SaaS product priced at $49/month. Growth stalling.

**Hypothesis:** Price might be "uncanny valley" — too high for impulse, too low for enterprise.

**Test:** A/B test with $99/month for new signups.

**Result:** Conversion dropped 20%, but revenue per signup increased 60%. Net positive.

**Additional Finding:** Higher price attracted more serious customers with better retention.

**Lesson:** Price is a product feature. It signals value and filters customers.

---

## Case Study 9: The Roadmap That Never Shipped

**Situation:** Annual roadmap committed to 47 features. Published to customers and board.

**Result:** By Q3, 70% of roadmap was obsolete. But team felt obligated to "hit commitments."

**Consequence:** Built features no one wanted. Missed emerging opportunities.

**Resolution:** Moved to outcome-based roadmaps. "We will improve X" not "We will build Y."

**Lesson:** Never commit to features, only to outcomes. Feature roadmaps become prison.

---

## Case Study 10: The Customer Council That Changed Direction

**Situation:** Product roadmap driven by sales team feature requests. "Customers are demanding X."

**Discovery:** Formed Customer Advisory Board (CAB). Talked to customers directly.

**Finding:** Sales reporting what would close deals, not what customers needed. Most "demanded" features unused.

**Resolution:** Direct customer research in product process. Sales input weighted, not final.

**Lesson:** Sales hears what closes deals. Product needs to understand what creates value.

# PART VI: 20 YEARS EXPERIENCE — FAILURE PATTERNS

## Failure Pattern 1: The Feature Factory

**Pattern:** Shipping features without validating they solve problems.

**Warning Signs:**
- Roadmap is list of features, not outcomes
- Success measured by "features shipped"
- No discovery before delivery
- PM writes specs, doesn't talk to customers

**Root Cause:** Output mindset vs. outcome mindset. Organization rewards visible activity.

**Prevention:** Outcome-based OKRs. Discovery before delivery. Customer evidence requirements.

---

## Failure Pattern 2: The HiPPO Trap

**Pattern:** Highest-Paid Person's Opinion overrides evidence.

**Warning Signs:**
- "Executive says we should..."
- Customer research ignored when inconvenient
- Strategy changes based on executive whim
- No data in decision meetings

**Root Cause:** Power dynamics override product process.

**Prevention:** Create culture of evidence. Require customer evidence for major decisions.

---

## Failure Pattern 3: The Premature Scale

**Pattern:** Scaling before achieving product-market fit.

**Warning Signs:**
- "We need more users to know if it works"
- Heavy marketing spend with poor retention
- Growing team faster than learning
- Vanity metrics celebrated

**Root Cause:** Pressure to show growth. Mistaking awareness for adoption.

**Prevention:** Nail it before you scale it. 40% "very disappointed" test before scaling.

---

## Failure Pattern 4: The One More Feature Fallacy

**Pattern:** "If we just add X, customers will convert/retain."

**Warning Signs:**
- Every sales call yields new feature request
- Competitors used to justify features
- Core product neglected for edge cases
- Settings page has 100+ options

**Root Cause:** Solving symptoms not root causes. Fear of focus.

**Prevention:** Understand job-to-be-done. Ask "why" not "what."

---

## Failure Pattern 5: The Sunk Cost Escalation

**Pattern:** Continuing failed initiatives because of past investment.

**Warning Signs:**
- "We've invested too much to stop now"
- Launch dates slip repeatedly
- Scope grows to "justify" investment
- Kill criteria undefined or ignored

**Root Cause:** Loss aversion. Emotional attachment to work.

**Prevention:** Pre-defined kill criteria. Regular project health reviews. Celebrate intelligent pivots.

# PART VII: 20 YEARS EXPERIENCE — SUCCESS PATTERNS

## Success Pattern 1: The Discovery Habit

**Pattern:** Continuous, lightweight customer research throughout development.

**Implementation:**
- Weekly customer touchpoints (PM + Designer + Engineer)
- Quick prototype tests before building
- Assumptions explicitly tracked
- Research insights broadly shared

**Conditions for Success:**
- User access mechanisms
- Protected time for discovery
- Leadership values learning

**Indicators:** Features ship with confidence. Pivot decisions made early. Few post-launch surprises.

---

## Success Pattern 2: The Outcome Roadmap

**Pattern:** Roadmap specifies outcomes, not features.

**Implementation:**
- "Improve activation rate" not "Build onboarding wizard"
- Teams empowered to find best solution
- Success measured by outcome, not output

**Conditions for Success:**
- Clear outcome metrics
- Empowered product teams
- Trust from leadership

**Indicators:** Roadmap stays relevant. Teams innovate on solutions. Better customer outcomes.

---

## Success Pattern 3: The Experiment Culture

**Pattern:** All significant decisions tested with experiments.

**Implementation:**
- Hypothesis required before build
- A/B testing infrastructure
- Statistical rigor (sample size, duration)
- Guardrail metrics protected

**Conditions for Success:**
- Sufficient traffic for tests
- Analytics infrastructure
- Comfort with uncertainty

**Indicators:** More decisions data-driven. Fewer big failures. Faster learning.

---

## Success Pattern 4: The Customer Advisory Board

**Pattern:** Structured ongoing relationship with representative customers.

**Implementation:**
- 10-15 customers across segments
- Quarterly calls/meetings
- Early access to concepts
- Input on roadmap priorities

**Conditions for Success:**
- Diverse customer selection
- Actual decision influence
- Ongoing relationship (not one-time)

**Indicators:** Better product decisions. Stronger customer relationships. Early warning system.

---

## Success Pattern 5: The Kill Criteria

**Pattern:** Pre-defined conditions for stopping initiatives.

**Implementation:**
- Before starting: "We will stop if X doesn't happen by Y"
- Regular checkpoints against criteria
- Celebrated pivots and stops
- Reallocate resources quickly

**Conditions for Success:**
- Leadership support for stopping
- No shame in stopping
- Resource flexibility

**Indicators:** Fewer zombie projects. Resources on high-value work. Faster overall velocity.

# PART VIII: 20 YEARS EXPERIENCE — WAR STORIES

## War Story 1: "The Board Is Asking About AI"

**Trigger:** When leadership asks to add AI to product without clear problem to solve.

**What I've Seen:** "AI" added to roadmap without use case. "Competitors have AI" cited as justification. AI features that add complexity without value.

**Response Protocol:**
1. Ask: "What customer problem are we solving?"
2. Propose: Customer research to identify AI opportunities
3. Suggest: Small experiments before big investments
4. If overruled: Document assumptions and success criteria

---

## War Story 2: "Sales Needs This Feature"

**Trigger:** When sales claims deal depends on specific feature.

**What I've Seen:** Features built for one deal, never used again. Product complexity increased for edge cases. "Critical" features that don't close deals anyway.

**Response Protocol:**
1. Talk to the customer directly (not just sales rep)
2. Understand the underlying job-to-be-done
3. Assess: Is this one customer or a pattern?
4. Propose alternative solutions if possible
5. If building, ensure broader customer value

---

## War Story 3: "Just Copy [Competitor]"

**Trigger:** When competitor feature cited as roadmap justification.

**What I've Seen:** Copying features that work for competitor's audience but not yours. Playing catch-up instead of differentiating. Building commodity features instead of innovations.

**Response Protocol:**
1. Understand why competitor built it (their context)
2. Validate whether your customers want it
3. If building, find way to differentiate
4. Consider: Is this core or commodity?

---

## War Story 4: "We Need To Move Fast"

**Trigger:** When timeline pressure eliminates discovery and testing.

**What I've Seen:** Speed over quality creating more rework. "Fast" launches that require months of fixes. Customer trust damaged by buggy releases.

**Response Protocol:**
1. Clarify: Speed to learn or speed to ship?
2. Propose: Minimum scope that enables learning
3. Show: Cost of wrong assumptions vs. cost of discovery
4. If overruled: Document risks and assumptions

---

## War Story 5: "The Data Says..."

**Trigger:** When data used to justify predetermined conclusion.

**What I've Seen:** Cherry-picked metrics. Correlation claimed as causation. Ignoring data that contradicts hypothesis. "Data-driven" as weapon against disagreement.

**Response Protocol:**
1. Examine data source and methodology
2. Ask about contradicting data
3. Distinguish correlation from causation
4. Require pre-registered hypotheses for experiments
5. Value qualitative alongside quantitative
