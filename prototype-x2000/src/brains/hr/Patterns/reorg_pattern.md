# Reorganization Pattern -- Organizational Restructuring Playbook

## Context

This pattern applies when the organization is changing its structure -- whether through growth (adding teams/departments), consolidation (merging teams), strategic pivot (reorganizing around new priorities), reduction in force (RIF), or M&A integration. Reorganizations are among the highest-risk HR activities: they affect employee livelihoods, team dynamics, productivity, and organizational trust. This pattern ensures that every reorganization is executed thoughtfully, legally, and humanely.

---

## Problem

Reorganizations are necessary for organizational health -- structures that served a 50-person company will break at 200. But reorganizations are frequently botched: unclear rationale, poor communication, inequitable impact, legal exposure, and prolonged uncertainty that destroys productivity and morale. Research from McKinsey suggests that only 23% of organizational redesigns achieve their intended objectives. This pattern dramatically improves the odds.

---

## Forces

- **Strategic necessity**: The business requires structural change to execute its strategy
- **Employee anxiety**: Reorganizations trigger fear of job loss, role change, and status disruption
- **Legal exposure**: RIFs and role eliminations create disparate impact risk; WARN Act obligations may apply
- **Productivity disruption**: Every reorganization creates a "J-curve" productivity dip (3-6 months)
- **Culture impact**: How a reorganization is executed signals organizational values
- **Information asymmetry**: Leadership knows the plan; employees speculate
- **Speed vs. thoroughness**: Moving too slowly prolongs uncertainty; moving too fast creates errors

## Solution: The Reorganization Process

### Phase 1: Strategic Assessment (Weeks 1-4)

**Step 1.1: Strategic rationale**
- Why is this reorganization necessary? What business problem does it solve?
- What has changed in the business environment that makes the current structure inadequate?
- What does the future state need to look like to execute strategy?
- Document the strategic rationale clearly -- it will be the foundation of all communications

**Step 1.2: Current state analysis**
- Map the current organizational structure (org chart, spans, layers, headcount by team)
- Identify what is working well (preserve these elements)
- Identify structural pain points (slow decisions, duplicated work, unclear ownership, coordination failures)
- Gather input from affected leaders (they understand operational reality that executive planning may miss)

**Step 1.3: Design criteria**
- Define criteria for evaluating structural options per `06_org_design/org_structure.md`:
  - Strategic alignment: Does this structure enable the strategy?
  - Customer focus: Does this structure serve customers effectively?
  - Decision speed: Does this structure enable fast, clear decisions?
  - Talent development: Does this structure develop leaders?
  - Scalability: Will this structure work at 2x the current size?
  - Cost efficiency: Does this structure eliminate unnecessary overhead?

**Step 1.4: Scenario planning**
- Develop 2-3 structural options
- Evaluate each against design criteria
- Model headcount, cost, and reporting implications for each
- Select preferred design with executive team
- Identify roles that are eliminated, created, or significantly changed

### Phase 2: People Impact Assessment (Weeks 4-6)

**Step 2.1: Role mapping**
- Map every current role to the new structure
- Categorize each role:
  - **Unchanged**: Same role, same team, same manager
  - **Modified**: Same person, changed scope, new team, or new manager
  - **Eliminated**: Role no longer exists in the new structure
  - **New**: Role that does not exist today; must be filled
- For eliminated roles: Identify whether affected employees can be placed in new or modified roles

**Step 2.2: Legal review (MANDATORY)**
- Engage employment counsel before any communication or action
- Adverse impact analysis: Does the reorganization disproportionately affect any protected group?
  - Analyze by age (ADEA/OWBPA), gender, race/ethnicity, disability status
  - Apply four-fifths rule to selection decisions
  - If disparate impact is identified, modify the plan before proceeding
- WARN Act assessment: Does the reorganization trigger WARN Act notice requirements?
  - 60-day advance written notice required if: 100+ employees affected at a single site
  - State WARN acts may have lower thresholds (e.g., CA WARN: 75 employees; NJ: 100 employees)
- Separation agreement review: Draft separation packages with appropriate OWBPA provisions

**Step 2.3: Selection criteria (if RIF)**
- If more employees exist than roles in the new structure, selection criteria must be:
  - Job-related (skills, performance, experience)
  - Consistently applied
  - Documented
  - Non-discriminatory
  - Reviewed by counsel

- Common selection criteria:
  - Performance ratings (last 2-3 review cycles)
  - Skills/competency match to new role requirements
  - Tenure (used only as tiebreaker; over-weighting tenure creates ADEA risk)
  - Business unit/function alignment

**Step 2.4: Separation package design**
- Severance: Typically 1-2 weeks per year of service (minimum 2 weeks)
- Health insurance: COBRA subsidy for 2-6 months
- Outplacement services: Resume support, job search coaching, career counseling
- Equity acceleration: Per plan terms (review with legal)
- Release/waiver: Separation agreement with release of claims (OWBPA-compliant for 40+ employees)
- Rehire eligibility: Clarify whether separated employees are eligible for future roles

### Phase 3: Communication Planning (Weeks 5-7)

**Step 3.1: Communication strategy**
- Who needs to know, in what order, with what level of detail?
- Cascade: Board/investors -> executive team -> affected managers -> affected employees -> broader organization -> external stakeholders
- Timeline: Minimize the gap between first disclosure and full communication (ideally same day)

**Step 3.2: Communication materials**
- All-hands presentation: Strategic rationale, what is changing, what is NOT changing, timeline, support resources
- Manager toolkit: Talking points, FAQ, escalation path for questions they cannot answer
- Individual notification scripts: For affected employees (role eliminated, role changed, role unchanged)
- Written FAQ document: Comprehensive answers to anticipated questions
- External communication: Press release, customer notification (if applicable)

**Step 3.3: Manager preparation**
- Brief all affected managers 24-48 hours before broader communication
- Provide manager toolkit and conduct role-play for difficult conversations
- Ensure every manager knows: what they can say, what they cannot say, and where to escalate
- Emphasize: empathy, clarity, and consistency

**Step 3.4: Communication principles**
- **Transparency**: Be as honest as possible about the rationale and impact
- **Empathy**: Acknowledge that this is difficult and uncertain
- **Clarity**: Be specific about what is changing and what is not
- **Consistency**: Every employee hears the same core message
- **Respect**: Treat departing employees with dignity; how you treat those leaving signals your values to those staying
- **Availability**: Leadership is visible and accessible for questions

### Phase 4: Execution (Weeks 7-10)

**Step 4.1: Notification day**
- Affected employees notified individually (in-person or video call if remote; NEVER by email alone)
- Notification conversation: Brief, clear, compassionate
  - State the decision directly ("Your position has been eliminated as part of the reorganization")
  - Explain the reason (business rationale, not personal performance)
  - Outline next steps (separation package, timeline, support)
  - Allow time for questions and emotions
  - Provide written summary and contact information for questions
- HR/HRBP present for all notifications (support for manager and employee)

**Step 4.2: Broader communication**
- All-hands meeting (same day as individual notifications)
- Leadership presents rationale, new structure, and path forward
- Open Q&A session (collect questions in advance and live)
- Follow up with written communication summarizing key points

**Step 4.3: Operational execution**
- System access changes (timing per legal guidance; balance security with dignity)
- Org chart and reporting updates in HRIS
- New role assignments communicated to remaining employees
- Transition plans for work previously done by departing employees
- Knowledge transfer sessions scheduled

**Step 4.4: Separation processing**
- Separation agreements distributed with required consideration periods (21 days individual; 45 days group/RIF with OWBPA disclosure)
- Final pay processed per state law (some states require immediate final pay)
- COBRA notices initiated
- Equipment return arranged
- Benefits continuation administered
- Outplacement services activated

### Phase 5: Stabilization (Weeks 10-22)

**Step 5.1: First 30 days**
- Daily check-in availability from HRBP for any employee with questions
- Manager 1:1s with every team member (address concerns, clarify new expectations)
- Quick pulse survey: "How are you feeling about the changes?" (5 questions max)
- Address any immediate issues or confusion rapidly

**Step 5.2: Days 30-60**
- Team workshops: Establish new team norms, working agreements, and goals
- Cross-functional alignment sessions (if dependencies changed)
- 60-day pulse survey: Are things settling? What needs attention?
- Performance expectations recalibrated for new structure and scope

**Step 5.3: Days 60-90**
- 90-day review of reorganization effectiveness
- Metrics assessment: Are the structural changes producing the intended outcomes?
- Employee engagement assessment: How has engagement been affected?
- Attrition monitoring: Is voluntary turnover spiking post-reorganization?
- Course corrections as needed

**Step 5.4: Ongoing monitoring**
- Monthly check-ins for first 6 months
- Quarterly review against strategic objectives that prompted the reorganization
- Annual assessment: Did the reorganization achieve its goals?

## Consequences

**Expected positive outcomes:**
- Organization structure aligned to current and future strategy
- Clear decision rights and accountability in new structure
- Departing employees treated with dignity and appropriate support
- Legal compliance maintained throughout
- Remaining employees understand the rationale and path forward

**Risks and mitigations:**
- Survivor guilt: Remaining employees feel guilt and anxiety; address through transparent communication and manager support
- Productivity dip: Expect 3-6 months of reduced productivity during transition; plan accordingly
- Voluntary attrition spike: Top performers may leave due to uncertainty; proactive retention conversations
- Legal claims: Disparate impact analysis and counsel review minimizes but does not eliminate risk
- Cultural damage: How the reorganization is executed becomes a cultural story; prioritize humanity

---

## Anti-Patterns (What NOT to Do)

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Better Approach |
|-------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Announce reorg without plan | Creates maximum anxiety with no clarity | Develop complete plan before any communication |
| Notify by email | Dehumanizing; signals lack of care | Always notify individually via live conversation |
| Eliminate roles without adverse impact analysis | Legal exposure; potential discrimination claims | Legal review BEFORE finalizing selection decisions |
| Restructure without changing processes | Same work done by different boxes on the org chart | Structure + process + decision rights must all change |
| Declare victory after Day 1 | Stabilization takes months | Monitor and support for 6+ months |
| Ignore remaining employees | They are anxious too; they will leave if unsupported | Invest heavily in communication, support, and re-engagement for the remaining team |

## Success Criteria

| Metric | Target | Measurement |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Legal claims | Zero | Legal tracking |
| WARN Act compliance | 100% | Legal/HR Ops verification |
| Adverse impact analysis clean | Four-fifths rule met for all groups | Statistical analysis |
| Communication satisfaction | >3.5/5.0 | Post-reorg pulse survey |
| 90-day voluntary attrition (remaining employees) | <10% annualized | HRIS |
| Strategic objectives progress at 6 months | On track | Business metrics |
| Employee engagement at 6 months | Within 10% of pre-reorg levels | Engagement survey |
