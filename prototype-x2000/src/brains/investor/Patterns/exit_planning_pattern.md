# Exit Planning Pattern — Maximizing Value at Company Exit

## Context

This pattern governs strategic planning and execution for company exits — whether through acquisition (M&A), initial public offering (IPO), or other liquidity events. Exit planning is not a reactive process triggered by inbound interest; it is a strategic discipline that should begin 2-3 years before the anticipated exit. Companies that plan their exits systematically achieve 20-40% higher valuations than those that respond opportunistically (Harvard Business Review, Fernandez 2019).

---

## Prerequisites

- [ ] Company has reached a stage where exit is a realistic near-to-medium-term outcome
- [ ] Board alignment on exit as a strategic objective (vs. continuing to build independently)
- [ ] Understanding of current market conditions for exits in the company's sector
- [ ] Financial advisor (investment banker) relationship established or in consideration
- [ ] Legal counsel with M&A or IPO experience engaged
- [ ] Cap table and waterfall analysis current

---

## Phase 1: Strategic Assessment (12-36 Months Pre-Exit)

### 1.1 Exit Path Evaluation

| Exit Path | When Appropriate | Typical Timeline | Typical Outcome |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Strategic acquisition | Strong strategic fit with larger company | 3-9 months | 3-10x revenue multiple |
| Financial acquisition (PE) | Profitable, stable growth, operational efficiency | 6-12 months | 2-5x revenue or 10-15x EBITDA |
| IPO | Large TAM, strong growth, $100M+ ARR | 18-24 months | Public market valuation |
| Direct listing | Strong brand, no capital needs | 12-18 months | Public market valuation |
| Merger of equals | Complementary companies, scale benefits | 6-12 months | Negotiated |
| Secondary sale (partial) | Founders/investors want partial liquidity | 1-6 months | Discount to last round |
| Management buyout | Founders want to buy back investor stakes | 3-9 months | Negotiated |

### 1.2 Readiness Assessment

Score the company's exit readiness:

| Dimension | Score (1-5) | Gap Analysis |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Revenue quality (growth, retention, predictability) | [ ] | [What needs to improve?] |
| Profitability trajectory | [ ] | [Path to profitability or current margins] |
| Customer concentration | [ ] | [No customer >10% of revenue?] |
| Team depth (non-founder-dependent) | [ ] | [Can the company run without founders?] |
| Intellectual property (protected, assigned) | [ ] | [Patents, trademarks, IP assignments] |
| Legal cleanliness | [ ] | [No pending litigation, clean corporate structure] |
| Financial reporting | [ ] | [Audited financials, clean accounting] |
| Market position | [ ] | [Category leader or clear #2?] |
| Operational scalability | [ ] | [Processes, systems, not just people] |
| Board and investor alignment | [ ] | [All parties aligned on exit timing and path] |

### 1.3 Value Maximization Roadmap

Based on the readiness assessment, build a 12-24 month roadmap to maximize exit value:

**Revenue Optimization:**
- Accelerate ARR growth (highest-impact valuation driver)
- Improve net revenue retention (120%+ NRR commands premium multiples)
- Reduce customer concentration (diversify revenue base)
- Demonstrate pricing power (expansion revenue, upsells)

**Profitability Optimization:**
- Demonstrate path to profitability (Rule of 40: growth + margin >= 40%)
- Improve gross margins to SaaS benchmarks (75%+ for software)
- Rationalize operating expenses where possible without hurting growth

**Operational Optimization:**
- Document key processes (reduce key-person risk)
- Implement SOC 2, ISO 27001, or relevant compliance certifications
- Clean up technical debt that would surface in due diligence
- Ensure all employee IP assignments are in place

## Phase 2: Market Preparation (6-18 Months Pre-Exit)

### 2.1 Investment Banker Selection (M&A Path)

**When to Engage a Banker:**
- Transaction expected to exceed $50M (below this, direct negotiation is common)
- Multiple potential acquirers (banker runs competitive process)
- Company lacks M&A experience (banker manages process and negotiation)

**Banker Selection Criteria:**
| Criterion | Weight | Assessment |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Sector expertise (relevant deals closed) | 30% | How many deals in our space? |
| Buyer relationships | 25% | Do they know the right corporate development teams? |
| Deal size fit | 20% | Is our deal in their sweet spot or too small? |
| Team quality (who actually works on the deal) | 15% | Will we get the partner or be handed to juniors? |
| Fee structure | 10% | Retainer + success fee, sliding scale by deal size |

**Typical Banker Fees:**

| Deal Size | Success Fee | Retainer |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| $25-50M | 3-5% | $25-75K/month |
| $50-100M | 2-3% | $50-100K/month |
| $100-500M | 1.5-2.5% | $75-150K/month |
| $500M+ | 1-1.5% | $100K+/month |

### 2.2 Potential Acquirer Mapping

Build a strategic buyer universe:

| Category | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Strategic buyers (adjacent) | Companies where your product fills a product gap | Platform company missing your feature |
| Strategic buyers (competitive) | Competitors who would benefit from consolidation | Direct competitors seeking market share |
| Strategic buyers (channel) | Companies where your product enhances their distribution | Company with your target customer base |
| Financial buyers (PE) | Private equity firms with relevant portfolio companies | Growth PE or tech-focused buyout firms |
| International buyers | Non-domestic companies seeking market entry | Foreign competitor or platform |

### 2.3 Building Acquirer Relationships

Start building relationships with potential acquirers 12-18 months before exit:
- Attend industry conferences where corporate development teams are present
- Explore partnership discussions (partnerships often precede acquisitions)
- Accept investor introductions to strategic companies
- Publish thought leadership that positions the company as category leader

## Phase 3: Transaction Execution (3-9 Months)

### 3.1 Process Types

| Process Type | Description | When to Use |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Broad auction | Banker contacts 20-50 potential buyers | Maximize price through competition |
| Targeted process | Banker contacts 5-10 pre-identified buyers | When buyer universe is small |
| Bilateral negotiation | Direct discussion with a single buyer | Strong inbound interest; relationship-driven |
| Pre-emptive offer | Buyer offers above-market price to avoid auction | Respond quickly; evaluate vs. running process |

### 3.2 M&A Process Timeline

| Week | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 1-2 | Engage banker; finalize materials (teaser, CIM, data room) |
| 3-4 | Banker contacts potential buyers; NDAs signed |
| 5-8 | Management presentations to interested buyers |
| 9-10 | First round bids (indications of interest — IOIs) |
| 11-14 | Select finalists; grant data room access for due diligence |
| 15-18 | Due diligence (financial, legal, technical, customer, HR) |
| 19-20 | Final bids (LOIs — letters of intent) |
| 21-24 | Negotiate definitive agreement |
| 25-28 | Sign definitive agreement |
| 29-36 | Regulatory approval (if required), HSR filing |
| 37-40 | Close transaction; wire funds |

### 3.3 Due Diligence Readiness

What acquirers will diligence:

| Area | Key Focus | Red Flags |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| Financial | Revenue quality, margins, cash flow, accounting policies | Irregular revenue recognition, unaudited books |
| Legal | IP ownership, contracts, litigation, compliance | Missing IP assignments, pending lawsuits |
| Technical | Architecture, technical debt, security, scalability | Legacy systems, security vulnerabilities |
| Customer | Concentration, satisfaction, churn, contracts | High churn, key customer about to leave |
| People | Key employees, comp, retention risk, culture | Key people likely to leave post-acquisition |
| Tax | Structure, transfer pricing, tax liabilities | Unpaid taxes, aggressive tax positions |
| Regulatory | Industry-specific compliance | Non-compliance with material regulations |

### 3.4 Negotiation Key Terms (M&A)

| Term | Founder Priority | Buyer Priority |
|------|-----------------|---------------|
| Purchase price | Higher | Lower |
| Cash vs. stock consideration | Cash (certainty) | Stock (alignment) |
| Escrow/holdback | Lower (5-10%) | Higher (10-20%) |
| Earnout | Avoid if possible | Performance-contingent payment |
| Retention packages | Fair for team | Sufficient to retain key people |
| Representations and warranties | Narrow, time-limited | Broad, long-lasting |
| Indemnification cap | Lower (10-15% of deal) | Higher (15-25%) |
| Non-compete | Shorter (1 year), narrow | Longer (2-3 years), broad |
| Closing conditions | Fewer, faster | More, protective |

## Phase 4: Post-Transaction (0-12 Months Post-Close)

### M&A Integration

| Priority | Action | Timeline |
|----------|--------|----------|
| Day 1 | Joint announcement; all-hands meeting; customer communication | Close day |
| Week 1 | Integration team established; 90-day plan shared | First week |
| Month 1 | Systems access, benefits transition, organizational alignment | 30 days |
| Month 3 | Product integration roadmap; initial operational integration | 90 days |
| Month 6 | Mid-point integration review; cultural alignment assessment | 180 days |
| Month 12 | Full integration complete; earnout first review (if applicable) | 365 days |

### Stakeholder Communication

| Stakeholder | Message | Timing | Channel |
|-------------|---------|--------|---------|
| Employees | Why this is good for them; what changes and what does not | Day 1 | All-hands + email |
| Customers | Continuity of service; enhanced capabilities; no disruption | Day 1 | Email + account manager outreach |
| Partners | Continued partnership; new opportunities | Day 1-3 | Direct outreach |
| Media | Joint press release | Day 1 | PR |
| Investors | Return distribution timeline and amounts | Day 1 | Direct communication |

## Common Failure Modes

| Failure | Prevention |
|---------|-----------|
| Reactive exit (no planning) | Begin exit planning 2-3 years ahead |
| Single buyer negotiation (no leverage) | Run competitive process or have credible alternatives |
| Due diligence surprises | Prepare data room 6+ months before process |
| Key employees leave during process | Retention packages and communication before close |
| Earnout disputes | Negotiate clear, objective earnout metrics; avoid subjective criteria |
| Cultural mismatch post-acquisition | Evaluate culture fit during buyer assessment phase |

---

## Post-Pattern Actions

1. Distribute proceeds per waterfall analysis
2. Communicate with all stakeholders per communication plan
3. Ensure tax obligations are understood by all recipients
4. Document exit learnings for institutional memory
5. Celebrate the team that built the company
