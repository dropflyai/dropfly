# Fundraising Pattern

Structured workflow for raising a venture capital funding round, from pre-seed through Series C. Covers preparation, investor targeting, pitching, negotiation, and closing.

---

## When to Use

- Raising a priced equity round (Seed, Series A, B, C)
- Raising a SAFE or convertible note round (Pre-seed, Bridge)
- Re-engaging fundraising after a failed attempt
- Evaluating whether to fundraise vs. bootstrap

## When NOT to Use

- Debt financing (bank loans, revenue-based financing) -- consult Finance Brain
- Grants or non-dilutive funding -- consult Finance Brain
- M&A or acqui-hire -- use separate M&A pattern
- Internal capital allocation -- use capital allocation framework in MBA curriculum

---

## Prerequisites

Before starting the Fundraising Pattern, verify:

- [ ] Clear reason for raising: what the capital will be used for and why now
- [ ] Business metrics that support the raise (stage-appropriate milestones)
- [ ] Founder alignment on dilution tolerance and valuation expectations
- [ ] At least 6 months of runway remaining (do not fundraise from desperation)
- [ ] Legal counsel engaged (startup-experienced attorney)
- [ ] Finance Brain consulted for financial model and cap table

## Phase 1: Prepare (4-8 weeks before first meeting)

### Objective
Build the fundraising materials, financial model, and target list before engaging investors.

### Activities

**1.1 Define the Raise**

| Parameter | Decision |
|-----------|----------|
| Round type | SAFE / Convertible Note / Priced Round |
| Target amount | $[X] (minimum viable) to $[X] (ideal) |
| Valuation range | $[X]M pre-money (floor) to $[X]M (target) |
| Use of funds | [Specific allocation: hiring, product, GTM, etc.] |
| Runway target | [X] months post-raise |
| Timeline | Target close by [date] |

**1.2 Build the Narrative**

The fundraising narrative must answer five questions:
1. **Why this market?** -- Large, growing, with a clear catalyst for change
2. **Why this team?** -- Unique insight, relevant experience, or unfair advantage
3. **Why now?** -- What has changed that makes this the right time
4. **Why this approach?** -- What is the insight that competitors are missing
5. **Why this round?** -- What milestones will this capital achieve

**1.3 Prepare Materials**

| Material | Purpose | Status |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Pitch deck (10-15 slides) | First meeting, email send | [ ] |
| Executive summary (1 page) | Cold outreach, forwardability | [ ] |
| Financial model (3-year) | Due diligence, deep dives | [ ] |
| Data room | Due diligence | [ ] |
| Cap table (current + pro forma) | Term sheet negotiation | [ ] |
| Product demo | Show, don't tell | [ ] |
| Customer references (3-5) | Social proof, diligence calls | [ ] |

**1.4 Pitch Deck Structure**

| Slide | Content | Time |
|-------|---------|------|
| 1. Title | Company name, one-line description, round info | 15s |
| 2. Problem | Pain point with specificity and evidence | 60s |
| 3. Solution | Your approach in simple terms | 60s |
| 4. Market | TAM/SAM/SOM with methodology | 45s |
| 5. Product | Demo or screenshots showing the product | 90s |
| 6. Traction | Key metrics, growth trajectory, customer logos | 60s |
| 7. Business Model | How you make money, unit economics | 60s |
| 8. Competition | Landscape positioning (2x2 matrix or value chain) | 45s |
| 9. Go-to-Market | Distribution strategy, channel economics | 45s |
| 10. Team | Founders + key hires, relevant experience | 45s |
| 11. Financials | Revenue projections, key assumptions, burn | 60s |
| 12. The Ask | Round size, use of funds, milestones | 45s |

**1.5 Build Investor Target List**

Create a tiered list of 40-60 investors:

| Tier | Count | Description | Approach |
|------|-------|-------------|----------|
| Tier 1 | 10-15 | Dream investors, highest conviction, best fit | Warm intro required |
| Tier 2 | 15-20 | Strong fit, good track record, likely interested | Warm intro preferred |
| Tier 3 | 15-25 | Acceptable fit, backup options | Warm or cold outreach |

For each investor, document:
- Fund name and partner name
- Investment thesis alignment
- Recent investments (check for conflicts)
- Check size range
- Warm introduction path (who can introduce you)
- Notes from prior interactions

**1.6 Practice**
- Rehearse pitch 10+ times (not optional)
- Do 3-5 practice pitches with friendly investors or advisors
- Incorporate feedback after each practice session
- Prepare answers for top 20 anticipated questions
- Practice handling objections without being defensive

## Phase 2: Pitch (4-8 weeks active fundraising)

### Objective
Generate investor interest, create competitive dynamics, and secure term sheets.

### Activities

**2.1 Outreach Cadence**

| Week | Activity |
|------|----------|
| Week 1-2 | Tier 2 and Tier 3 outreach (warm-up, refine pitch based on reactions) |
| Week 3-4 | Tier 1 outreach (pitch is now refined, momentum building) |
| Week 4-6 | Follow-ups, partner meetings, due diligence |
| Week 6-8 | Term sheet negotiation, closing |

**Why start with Tier 2/3 first:** Your pitch improves with repetition. Do not waste your dream investors on your worst version of the pitch.

**2.2 Meeting Management**

After every investor meeting, log:

| Field | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| Investor | [Name, Fund] |
| Date | YYYY-MM-DD |
| Meeting type | Intro / Partner meeting / Due diligence |
| Interest level | Hot / Warm / Cold / Pass |
| Key questions asked | [List] |
| Objections raised | [List] |
| Next step | [Specific action and date] |
| Follow-up needed | [What to send/do] |

**2.3 Creating Competitive Dynamics**

- Run a tight process: 6-8 weeks, not 6 months
- Create soft deadlines ("we're targeting to close by [date]")
- When you have genuine interest from multiple investors, communicate this honestly
- Never fabricate competing term sheets (it will destroy your reputation)
- Schedule partner meetings close together to create urgency

**2.4 Handling Common Objections**

| Objection | Response Framework |
|-----------|-------------------|
| "It's too early" | Acknowledge stage, redirect to traction trajectory and team strength |
| "The market is too small" | Challenge their sizing methodology, show expansion path |
| "What about [competitor]?" | Differentiation + why you win in your segment |
| "The team is missing [role]" | Acknowledge, show hiring plan, demonstrate awareness |
| "Unit economics don't work" | Show path to profitability at scale with cohort data |
| "Come back when you have X" | Ask what specifically would change their mind, log for future |

|----------|--------------|-----------------|---------------|------------|--------|
| [Name] | [Date] | [Date] | [Date] | [Date] | [Stage] |

## Phase 3: Negotiate (1-3 weeks)

### Objective
Secure the best possible terms while maintaining investor relationships.

### Activities

**3.1 Term Sheet Evaluation**

When evaluating a term sheet, prioritize these elements:

| Element | Priority | What to Watch |
|---------|----------|---------------|
| Valuation (pre-money) | High | Determines dilution. Compare to comparable rounds. |
| Liquidation preference | Critical | 1x non-participating is standard. Anything else is a red flag. |
| Board composition | Critical | Founder control matters. Odd number, founder majority. |
| Pro-rata rights | Medium | Standard for lead investors. Understand dilution implications. |
| Anti-dilution | Medium | Broad-based weighted average is standard. Full ratchet is toxic. |
| Vesting | Medium | 4-year with 1-year cliff is standard for new grants. |
| Protective provisions | High | Read carefully. Limit investor veto power on operations. |
| Participation rights | Critical | Non-participating preferred is standard. Participating is expensive at exit. |
| Option pool | High | Size and whether it is pre-money or post-money matters for dilution. |
| Drag-along | Medium | Standard but read the threshold carefully. |

**3.2 Negotiation Principles**

1. **Know your BATNA** (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement). Multiple term sheets strengthen your position.
2. **Focus on the 3-4 terms that actually matter.** Do not negotiate every line item -- it signals inexperience.
3. **Separate the economic terms from the control terms.** Be more flexible on economics, less flexible on control.
4. **Be direct and honest.** "We have another term sheet" only if you actually do.
5. **Use your lawyer for legal terms, negotiate economic terms yourself.**
6. **Speed matters.** Once you have a term sheet you like, move quickly. Delay kills deals.

**3.3 Multiple Term Sheets**

If you have multiple term sheets:
- Compare side by side on all key terms (not just valuation)
- Consider investor value-add beyond capital (network, expertise, brand)
- Communicate honestly with all parties about your timeline
- Make a decision within 1-2 weeks -- do not string investors along

### Decision Gate 3: Term Sheet Acceptance

| Criteria | Threshold | Actual | Pass? |
|----------|-----------|--------|-------|
| Valuation within acceptable range | Yes | [Y/N] | Y/N |
| Liquidation preference 1x non-participating | Yes | [Y/N] | Y/N |
| Board composition acceptable | Yes | [Y/N] | Y/N |
| No toxic terms (full ratchet, participating preferred, excessive liquidation) | None | [Y/N] | Y/N |
| Investor value-add assessed | Yes | [Y/N] | Y/N |
| Legal counsel reviewed | Yes | [Y/N] | Y/N |

**Gate result:** SIGN term sheet / NEGOTIATE specific terms / DECLINE and continue fundraising

## Phase 4: Close (2-6 weeks after term sheet)

### Objective
Complete due diligence, finalize legal documents, and wire funds.

### Activities

**4.1 Due Diligence Preparation**

Ensure data room contains:
- [ ] Corporate documents (articles, bylaws, cap table, prior funding docs)
- [ ] Financial statements (audited if available, otherwise management-prepared)
- [ ] Customer contracts and pipeline
- [ ] IP documentation (patents, trademarks, assignments)
- [ ] Employee agreements (at-will, IP assignment, non-compete)
- [ ] Material contracts (vendors, partners, leases)
- [ ] Insurance policies
- [ ] Any litigation or regulatory issues

**4.2 Legal Process**

| Document | Owner | Status |
|----------|-------|--------|
| Stock Purchase Agreement (SPA) | Investor's counsel drafts | [ ] |
| Investor Rights Agreement (IRA) | Investor's counsel drafts | [ ] |
| Voting Agreement | Investor's counsel drafts | [ ] |
| Right of First Refusal (ROFR) | Investor's counsel drafts | [ ] |
| Board consent | Company counsel prepares | [ ] |
| Stockholder consent | Company counsel prepares | [ ] |
| Amended Certificate of Incorporation | Company counsel prepares | [ ] |
| Legal opinion | Company counsel provides | [ ] |

**4.3 Common Closing Delays**

| Delay | Prevention |
|-------|-----------|
| Missing employee IP assignments | Audit and fix before fundraising starts |
| Cap table discrepancies | Clean up cap table before fundraising |
| Prior investor consent required | Engage prior investors early in process |
| Tax or entity structure issues | Address with counsel before fundraising |
| Key customer or contract issues | Resolve or disclose proactively |

**4.4 Post-Close**

- [ ] Wire received and confirmed
- [ ] Board restructured per new agreement
- [ ] 409A valuation scheduled (within 30 days of close)
- [ ] Option pool updated in cap table management tool
- [ ] Investor added to board communications
- [ ] Investor update template established (monthly or quarterly)
- [ ] Press release or announcement (if desired)
- [ ] Thank warm introducers who made connections
- [ ] Update all investors who passed (maintain relationship for future rounds)

## Kill Criteria

| Condition | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| 30+ meetings with zero partner meetings | Pause. Re-evaluate pitch, market, or timing. |
| Systemic objection that cannot be addressed | Pause. Address the objection before continuing. |
| Runway drops below 3 months during fundraise | Switch to bridge financing or cost-cutting mode. |
| Only term sheets with toxic terms | Decline. Reduce burn rate and try again in 6 months. |
| Founder disagreement on terms or strategy | Resolve founder alignment before proceeding. |

---

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Fundraising too early.** Raising before you have stage-appropriate metrics leads to either failure or terrible terms.
2. **Not running a tight process.** A 6-month fundraise signals weakness. Aim for 6-8 weeks of active meetings.
3. **Optimizing only for valuation.** Terms, board composition, and investor quality matter as much or more than headline valuation.
4. **Not practicing the pitch.** Your first pitch will be your worst. Do not waste it on your top-choice investor.
5. **Taking the first term sheet out of desperation.** If you have runway, you have time to get the right deal.
6. **Neglecting the business during fundraising.** Metrics declining during a fundraise is devastating. Assign someone to run the business.
7. **Not reading the legal documents.** "My lawyer will handle it" is not sufficient. Founders must understand every term they sign.
8. **Burning bridges with investors who pass.** A "no" today can be a "yes" next round. Always be gracious.
